## How It Works

### Section Title
Three Steps to a Plan That Bends Around You

### Step 1: Tell Us About Your Goal
Pick your race distance and when you want to run it. Choose your experience level or let the app figure it out from your running history. Select how many days a week you can run and how much time you actually have — including shower time. That's it.

### Step 2: Get a Plan Built on Research
Your plan is generated using evidence from peer-reviewed studies and established coaching methods — not a one-size-fits-all template. It follows proper periodization (Base → Build → Peak → Taper), uses smart mileage progression, and respects recovery science.

### Step 3: Life Happens. The Plan Adapts.
Missed a run? The app reschedules intelligently — no guilt, no penalty. Going on vacation? Tell the app and it redistributes your training or eases you back in. Holiday coming up? The plan adjusts automatically. You don't manage the plan. The plan manages itself around you.

### Feature 2: Smart Mileage Progression

#### Headline
The 10% Rule Is Outdated. We Use Better Science.

#### Copy
The "10% rule" — increase weekly mileage by no more than 10% — has been the default advice for decades. But recent research shows it's an oversimplification that doesn't actually predict injury well.

We use an adaptive stepped progression model calibrated to your current volume. If you're running low mileage, you can safely increase faster. At high mileage, the app automatically dials back the rate. And every few weeks, you get a planned deload to let your body absorb the training.

#### Research Callout
> A 2012 Aarhus University study found that 47 uninjured novice runners averaged 22.1% weekly volume increases — more than double the 10% rule — without injury. Injured runners had increases over 30%.

> A 2025 BJSM cohort study of 5,200 runners found that single-session spikes — not weekly mileage jumps — are the better predictor of injury. Our single-session spike guard caps any individual run at 110% of your longest run in the past 30 days.

#### Key Points
- Adaptive increase rates: up to 15% at low volume, 7% at high volume
- Configurable deload patterns: linear, 2-up-1-down, 3-up-1-down, 4-up-1-down
- Single-session spike guard based on the latest injury research (2025 BJSM, n=5,200)
- Bone remodeling science: Jack Daniels' equilibrium method — increase, then hold for 3-4 weeks before the next jump

### Feature 3: Polarized Training (80/20)

#### Headline
80% Easy. 20% Hard. Zero Junk Miles.

#### Copy
The most common training mistake? Running too hard on easy days and too easy on hard days. You end up stuck in a "gray zone" that doesn't build fitness efficiently.

Our app tracks your intensity distribution across every session using a 3-zone model. It flags when you're drifting into the gray zone and coaches you back — gently. The result is faster adaptation, fewer injuries, and better race-day performance.

#### Research Callout
> Dr. Stephen Seiler's landmark research, confirmed by multiple meta-analyses (including a 2024 PMC review), shows that an 80/20 easy-to-hard ratio produces the best endurance outcomes across all levels. A study of Ironman athletes found r=0.94 correlation between gray zone training and slower race times.

#### Key Points
- 3-zone model: Easy (Zone 1), Threshold (Zone 2), High Intensity (Zone 3)
- Real-time gray zone detection with coaching nudges
- Intensity dashboard showing your actual distribution vs targets
- Phase-adjusted targets: more easy in base phase, more intensity in build/peak
- Works in both VDOT mode (pace-based) and RPE mode (effort-based)

Source label: Seiler et al., 2024 PMC meta-analysis.

### Feature 6: Age-Adjusted Training

#### Headline
Your Body at 50 Is Not Your Body at 25. Your Plan Shouldn't Be Either.

#### Copy
Recovery takes longer as you age. Tendons adapt more slowly. VO2max declines. But that doesn't mean you can't train hard — you just need smarter recovery timing and adjusted progression.

Our app adjusts plans based on your age bracket with changes grounded in exercise physiology — not guesswork.

#### Key Points
- Age brackets: Under 40, 40-49, 50-59, 60+
- Longer recovery windows: 24-36 hours (under 40) → 48-72+ hours (60+)
- Extended warm-ups for older runners (10-15 min vs 5 min)
- Protein targets by age (1.6g/kg for under-40, up to 2.0g/kg for 60+)
- Non-standard 9-10 day training cycles for 60+ runners — more recovery days without fewer quality sessions
- Returning masters runners: gradual ramp-back protocol starting at 50-60% of previous volume

#### Research Callout
> VO2max declines roughly 0.5-1% per year from age 35, accelerating after 60. But research shows no notable decline in training volume, cardiovascular performance, or running economy in masters athletes who maintain consistent training. Slower recovery, not less capability, is what changes.

### Feature 9: Taper Protection

#### Headline
The Hardest Part of Training Is Doing Less. We've Got You.

#### Copy
Taper anxiety is real. You've trained for months, and suddenly the plan says "run less." Every runner's instinct screams "do more." But the science is clear: runners who stick to their taper perform better.

So we lock it. You can't add volume during taper. You can skip, reduce, or swap hard for easy — but you can't panic-add miles. The app explains why, cites the research, and gently reminds you that the work is already done.

#### Research Callout
> Bosquet et al. (2007) meta-analysis found that a 41-60% volume reduction over 2 weeks is optimal for endurance athletes. Runners who stick to their taper finish approximately 2.6% faster than those who add extra training.

- Weekly summaries focus on what you did, not what you missed

### Feature 12: Weather-Adjusted Pacing

#### Headline
Your Pace Target Changes When the Weather Does

#### Copy
Running a 5:30/km pace at 15°C is not the same effort as running it at 32°C. Your body works harder in the heat — and your pace targets should reflect that. The app checks the weather before every outdoor run and adjusts your targets automatically. No more beating yourself up for running "slow" on a hot day.

#### Key Points
- Pace adjusted approximately 1.5 sec/km per degree Celsius above 15°C
- Humidity scaling on top of temperature adjustment
- Weather snapshot shown on the daily calendar view
- Clear messaging: "Pace adjusted for 32°C heat — your effort is the same"

## The Science Behind the App

### Section Title
Built on Research, Not Bro Science

### Copy
Every algorithm in this app traces back to published research, peer-reviewed studies, or established coaching methodology. Here's a sample of what's under the hood:

### Research Cards

**Mileage Progression**
Adaptive stepped model based on Aarhus University (2012) study of novice runner injury rates and Jack Daniels' equilibrium method. The outdated 10% rule is replaced with volume-calibrated progression rates.

**Injury Prevention**
Single-session spike guard based on a 2025 British Journal of Sports Medicine cohort study (n=5,200). ACWR (acute:chronic workload ratio) monitoring for proactive risk scoring.

**Taper Science**
Bosquet et al. (2007) meta-analysis — 41-60% volume reduction over 2 weeks for optimal race-day performance. Fixed taper lengths by distance, locked against user modification.

**Polarized Training**
Dr. Stephen Seiler's 80/20 research, confirmed by 2024 PMC meta-analysis. 3-zone intensity model with gray zone detection. Phase-specific intensity targets.

**Periodization**
Base → Build → Peak → Taper → Recovery model drawn from Hal Higdon, BAA, and Scientific Triathlon's interview with coach John Davis on modern marathon periodization.

**Beginner Progression**
Jeff Galloway's run/walk method (98% marathon finish rate). C25K dropout analysis informing our 50% max increase cap between weeks.

**Recovery Science**
Age-specific recovery windows based on exercise physiology research. PEACE & LOVE protocols for acute injury management. 1-easy-day-per-mile-raced post-race recovery (Galloway guideline).

**Weather Adjustments**
Pace targets adjusted approximately 1.5 sec/km per degree Celsius above 15°C, scaling with humidity. Based on established heat performance models.
